Try to identify the trend and seasonal component in the time series¶

Use the "naive decomposition" implemented in seasonal_decompose¶

In [9]:
result = seasonal_decompose(uva, model='additive')
In [10]:
plt.rc('figure',figsize=(16,12))
plt.rc('font',size=13)
In [11]:
result.plot();

This method already identifies a seasonal pattern and it may just as well correspond to the upticks observed before the end of the calendar year and around midyear.

However, this method is not robust to outliers and in N26's case, these may occur due to various product and/or marketing initiatives. Therefore, one can also use STL (Seasonal Trend decomposition using Locally estimated scatterplot smoothing), which carries further advantages over simple decomposition.

Use STL decomposition¶

Use both a robust and non-robust decomposition for comparison.

In [12]:
def add_stl_plot(fig, res, legend):
    """Add 3 plots from a second STL fit"""
    axs = fig.get_axes()
    comps = ['trend', 'seasonal', 'resid']
    for ax, comp in zip(axs[1:], comps):
        series = getattr(res, comp)
        if comp == 'resid':
            ax.plot(series, marker='o', linestyle='none')
        else:
            ax.plot(series)
            if comp == 'trend':
                ax.legend(legend, frameon=False)
In [13]:
stl = STL(uva, period = 12, robust=True)
res_robust = stl.fit()
fig = res_robust.plot()
res_non_robust = STL(uva, period = 12, robust=False).fit()
add_stl_plot(fig, res_non_robust, ['Robust','Non-robust'])

The above robust (blue series) decomposition may be interesting to interpret and make sense of, however:

  • the trend component is linear
  • within a 12 month period we observe two peaks in the seasonal component: one before the end of the year and another one right after the middle of a calendar year
  • the above mentioned two peaks have a balancing evolution: while the end of the year peak is decreasing over time, the midyear peak is increasing over time
  • the residuals have rather high values especially for dates around the seasonal peaks, however for data closer to the present their values are smaller and smaller.

Here the advantage of STL can be observed, in that it allows the seasonal component to vary over time and produces a stable, linear trend unlike seasonal_decompose which forces a stable seasonal component.

This is particularly valuable in the context of shifting consumer patterns.

Particularly, as product tiers have changed over time (i.e. benefits have been concentrated in the upper tiers, especially travel-related benefits), so might have consumers' behaviors adapted to these changes and to the immediate benefits and value brought by these tiers to them.

It will be worth revisiting this analysis in 6 months' time for re-evaluation and see if the observations made will hold.

In this situation, we can see that the non-robust (sensitive to outliers) trend line has a higher variation.
The robust method, however, shows a slightly decreasing trend with virtually no distinguishable seasonality and higher residuals.

Conclusion¶

There seems to be a seasonal component in the upgrade behavior when analyzing absolute volumes: two peaks (one around mid year increasing in amplitude over time, and another one before the end of the year decreasing in amplitude over time) can explain some of the ups and downs observed in our data.
There doesn't seem to be any distinguishable seasonality when analyzing the share of standard MAUs that upgrade.

Combining the two above might lead to the conclusion that the upgrade rate has no distinguishable seasonality, but the absolute volumes follow new user acquisition/MAU activation trends.
